Best Gaming PC Builds 2023

Ethereum’s move to proof-of-stake in 2022 was the final nail in the coffin for cryptomining, and we finally started seeing GPUs selling at MSRP again. But it’s not only GPUs, low demand and excess inventory has lead to falling prices on SSDs and RAM. The time is ripe to build a PC, so here are some of the best bang-for-buck machines you can build right now.


Super-Budget PC: $519

CPU: Intel Core i3 12100F (bundled cooler) $84 Newegg
Motherboard: MSI PRO H610M-G DDR4 $90 Newegg
GPU: ZOTAC Gaming GeForce GTX 1650 AMP CORE $170 Amazon
RAM: Corsair Vengeance LPX 16GB (2 X 8GB) DDR4 3200 C16 $38 Amazon
Storage: Crucial P3 Plus 500GB PCIe 4.0 $30 Amazon
Chassis: Cooler Master MasterBox Q300L Micro-ATX Tower $67 Amazon
PSU:Thermaltake Smart 500W 80+ White Certified $40 Amazon

The i3 12100F is arguably the best bang for buck CPU in existence, and at $85 it’s an absolute steal. It can keep up with some 6c/12t chips in productivity and the gaming performance is immense. The newer 13100F only offers a minor boost in base/boost speed without the architectural improvements of Rocket Lake, so unless you can find a good deal, stick with the 12100F. Paying $40 extra for a minor bump in clock frequency isn’t worth it. The Ryzen 5 5500 is a decent alternative for this build, but cannot quite match the 12100F’s performance and value.

The GTX 1650 is a very popular GPU and for good reason: it offers medium to high 1080p gaming at an affordable price, and can push over 200FPS in eSports titles like CSGO and Valorant. The RX 6500 XT isn’t the greatest card ever, but at $170 it deserves a mention. Our H610M motherboard does support PCIe 4.0, so we shouldn’t experience any issues with its PCIe x4 bandwidth limitation. One reason I’m leaning towards the 1650 is hardware encoding.

We make use of the H610M’s single PCIe 3.0 x4 M.2 slot by going for the Crucial P3 Plus, very attractively priced at $32. Remember to enable XMP in BIOS so our Corsair Vengeance RAM runs at 3200MHz. Apart from gaming, our machine is capable of basic creator workloads and should be able to cope with 1080p 30FPS streaming. It’s not going to win any benchmark awards, but it’ll definitely keep the casual gamer appeased.

Recommended monitor: MSI 24.0 75 Hz VA FHD Monitor 5 ms


Budget PC: $806

CPU: Intel Core i5 12400F (bundled cooler) $149 Newegg
Motherboard: GIGABYTE B660M DS3H Micro ATX $110 Newegg
GPU: ASRock Challenger D Radeon RX 6650 XT 8GB $280 Newegg
RAM: TeamGroup T-Force Vulcan Z DDR4 32GB 3200MHz CL16 $60 Amazon
Storage: Crucial P3 Plus 1TB PCIe 4.0 $52 Amazon
Chassis: Cooler Master MasterBox Q300L Micro-ATX Tower $60 Amazon
PSU: EVGA SuperNOVA 650W Modular $95 Newegg

Like the 12100F, the 12400F is another standout value offering in Alder Lake. We’re upping the core count from 4 to 6, giving us a good balance between gaming and productivity. The new 13400F is quite tempting as it comes with an additional four E-cores, but at $205 it’s a whopping $60 more than the 12400F, which is a bit too much to pay for 15% better multithreaded performance. Likewise the i5 12600K is a solid option at $224, offering even better performance than the 13400F. The closest AMD competitor for this build is the Ryzen 5 7600 at $228, but that will require a pricey B650 motherboard and DDR5. These chips don’t make sense on a tight budget, but they’re definitely viable for a sub $1000 build.

The RX 6650 XT is unbeatable at $260 and it’s not even close. It offers a high refresh rate 1080p experience and playable frame rates at 1440p. The RTX 3060 gets an honourable mention at $350, but the only real benefit is better ray tracing, which isn’t a priority at this budget. The RX 6650 XT comfortably beats the RTX 3060 when it comes to rasterisation. Another card I’m eyeing is the unreleased RTX 4060. If Nvidia isn’t overly greedy with pricing, it could open up smooth 1440p gaming for this build.

We’re sticking with DDR4 as the increase in price for DDR5 doesn’t justify the minor performance boost, and 32GB should be plenty. The Crucial P3 Plus gives us blazingly fast PCIe 4.0 storage with up to 5000MB/s sequential reads. This build just screams value!

Recommended monitor: MSI Optix G24C 24″ 144Hz


Mid-Range PC: $1572

CPU: Intel i5 13600K $319 Amazon
Cooler: DeepCool AK620 High-Performance CPU Cooler $65 Newegg
Motherboard: GIGABYTE B760M DS3H Micro ATX $140 Newegg
GPU: ASRock Phantom Gaming Radeon RX 6950 XT 16GB $599 Amazon
RAM: Corsair Vengeance RGB DDR5 32GB 6000MHz C30 $145 Amazon
Storage: Samsung 980 Pro SSD 1TB PCIe 4.0 $90 Amazon
Chassis: Corsair 4000D Airflow 104 Amazon
PSU: EVGA SuperNOVA 750W Modular $110 Newegg

With an increased budget, we’re embracing new tech in the form of Raptor Lake and DDR5. The 14-core 13600K is going to crush any productivity task we throw at it, and at $320 it presents outstanding value. AMD is off the pace here, as the closest Zen 4 rival is the Ryzen 7 7700X at $348, and that chip is actually slower than the 13600K. The comparison looks even worse when you consider the platform cost of AM5.

The RX 6950 XT is excellent value at $599, and it will give us a high refresh rate 1440p experience and over 80FPS in 4K. The new RTX 4070 is the biggest Nvidia competitor here at $599, although it cannot match the 6950 XT in rasterisation. It might be a more attractive proposition once the price drops below MSRP.

Unless you’re going to run Cinebench all day, a dual tower air cooler should keep our 13600K in check. The AK620 is the obvious choice as it offers excellent performance and quality. The rest of the build is fairly standard stuff, with 32GB of DDR5 running at 6000MHz. We have high-speed storage in the Samsung 980 Pro, one of the best PCIe 4.0 hard drives you’ll find.

This build is going to be an absolute powerhouse when it comes to gaming and productivity workloads.

Recommended monitor: GIGABYTE G27Q 27″ 144Hz 1440p or LG UltraGear 27GN95B-B 27″ 144Hz 4K


High-End PC: $3548

CPU: Ryzen 9 7950X $575 Amazon
Cooler: ARCTIC Liquid Freezer II 240 RGB $123 Newegg
Motherboard: ASUS ROG Strix X670E-A Gaming $392 Amazon
GPU: GIGABYTE Gaming GeForce RTX 4090 24GB $1700 Newegg
RAM: G.Skill Trident Z5 NEO RGB Series 64GB DDR5 6000MHz C30 $265 Amazon
Storage: WD_BLACK 2TB SN850X NVMe PCIe 4.0 $193 Amazon
Chassis: Lian Li Lancool III RGB $150 Newegg
PSU: Corsair RM850x 850W Modular PSU $150 Amazon

We’re throwing caution to the wind with our high-end build, as we’re more focused on performance than budget. I’m really liking the Ryzen 9 7950X, reason being the AM5 platform will be supported until 2025. This machine is futureproof and there is potential for drop-in upgrades. The RTX 4090 is an absolute behemoth and will give us over 140FPS in 4K gaming. Even with ray tracing enabled we’ll still be pushing 80FPS in most titles.

We’ve taken no half measures on storage and RAM: the Gen4 SN850X is crazy fast at up to 7300 MB/s reads, and we’re running the Trident Z5 Neo at C30 and 6000MHz.

While the 7950X is designed to run at the thermal limit of 95 degrees, a beefy cooler is still recommended to tame the beast. The Arctic Liquid Freezer II is a reliable AIO and the Lian Li Lancool III case allows us to do a top mount.

Recommended monitor: GIGABYTE 32″ 144Hz 4K Curved Gaming Monitor
